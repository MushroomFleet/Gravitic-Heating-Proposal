# Gravitic Heating: A Grounding Document for an Unmodelled Forcing in Earth's Climate System

**Status:** Hypothesis grounding — research synthesis

**Subject:** Variable gravitational forcing on Earth's interior as an unaccounted driver of long-term climate variability  
**Context:** Proposed to address the 30-year gap between climate model outputs and paleoclimate proxy observations

---

## Abstract

Standard climate models attribute observed temperature changes primarily to greenhouse gas radiative forcing, solar insolation variability (Milankovitch cycles), volcanic aerosols, and ocean-atmosphere feedbacks. However, a persistent and acknowledged gap exists between model outputs and the physical proxy record — most notably the Holocene temperature conundrum, the 100,000-year glacial cycle problem, and an unresolved ~27 TW thermal deficit in Earth's internal heat budget. This document grounds the hypothesis that variable gravitational forcing — generated by the cyclic interplay of the Sun, Moon, and outer planets acting on Earth's differentiated interior — constitutes a meaningful and systematically unmodelled climate forcing. Evidence is drawn from solar system body research, Earth's internal heat budget, core-mantle coupling observations, and confirmed chaos in planetary orbital mechanics.

## 1. Background: The Modelling Gap

### 1.1 What the Models Do

General circulation models (GCMs) and Earth System Models (ESMs) used in IPCC assessments account for the following forcing mechanisms:

- **Solar insolation** — changes in Earth's orbital geometry (eccentricity, obliquity, precession) that alter the distribution of incoming solar radiation across latitudes and seasons
- **Greenhouse gas forcing** — radiative effects of CO₂, CH₄, N₂O, and other gases
- **Volcanic aerosols** — episodic stratospheric loading from major eruptions
- **Ice-albedo feedback** — changes in surface reflectivity as ice sheets expand or contract
- **Ocean heat capacity and thermohaline circulation** — slow redistribution of heat through ocean overturning

### 1.2 The Acknowledged Failures

Despite decades of refinement, these models produce systematic errors against the physical record:

**The Holocene Temperature Conundrum**  
Proxy reconstructions using 1,310 pollen records, ocean sediment cores, and lake sediment records consistently show that Northern Hemisphere temperatures peaked approximately 7,000 years ago and have been cooling since — consistent with declining boreal summer insolation driven by orbital precession. Climate models, by contrast, simulate a long-term Holocene *warming* trend driven by rising greenhouse gas concentrations and retreating ice sheets. The two accounts produce *opposite signs* for the direction of temperature change during the same period. This discrepancy — known as the Holocene temperature conundrum — has been open and unresolved since the 1990s.

**The 100,000-Year Problem**  
For the past 800,000 years, glacial cycles have followed a dominant ~100,000-year periodicity matching Earth's eccentricity cycle. Before approximately 1 million years ago, the dominant periodicity was ~41,000 years, matching obliquity. There was no identified significant change in orbital forcing to explain this transition. The shift arose from within the Earth system itself. No existing model satisfactorily explains why it happened.

**The Proxy Uncertainty Floor**  
The median uncertainty across all Holocene paleoclimate proxy records is ±1.7°C. Individual proxy types reach ±3°C. The entire anthropogenic warming signal being attributed to human activity since 1850 is approximately 1.5°C — a value that sits *inside the error bar of the measurement tool used to establish its historical context*. Peer-reviewed literature has explicitly noted that IPCC statements claiming current temperatures are unprecedented over multi-century periods are statistically invalid because the mean and variance of intervals of different lengths cannot be properly compared using the available data.

**The Thermal Deficit**  
Earth's surface heat flux is approximately 47 TW. Internal radiogenic heat production from the mantle and crust is estimated at approximately 20 TW. This leaves an acknowledged deficit of approximately 27 TW that standard models do not fully account for. This gap is real, published, and without a fully accepted explanation.

## 2. The Hypothesis: Gravitic Interior Heating

### 2.1 Core Proposition

The Sun, Moon, and outer planets exert gravitational forces on Earth that are not uniform across the planet's interior. Because Earth is a differentiated body — with a liquid outer core, solid inner core, semi-viscous mantle, and rigid lithosphere, each at different densities — differential gravitational acceleration across these layers generates internal deformation, friction, and heat. This process, broadly termed **gravitic interior heating** or **gravitational shearing**, is modulated by:

- Earth's orbital eccentricity (the sun-Earth distance varies)
- The positions and alignments of the outer planets, primarily Jupiter and Saturn, which perturb Earth's orbit
- The Moon's orbital eccentricity and inclination
- The solar system's barycentric motion — the centre of mass of the entire solar system moves, and the Sun itself orbits it in response to planetary mass distribution

The key claim is that this forcing is *variable* over time — it is not a constant background term that can simply be subtracted — and that its variation on timescales of thousands to hundreds of thousands of years may contribute meaningfully to internal heat output, modulating mantle convection rates, seafloor spreading, submarine volcanism, and ocean-floor CO₂ outgassing.

### 2.2 Distinction from Conventional Tidal Heating

Conventional tidal heating refers to the deformation of a body's *surface* by gravitational gradients — the ocean tides raised by the Moon are the familiar example. Solid Earth tidal dissipation (deformation of the rock itself) is also known and contributes approximately 0.1 TW — generally considered negligible and often excluded from thermal history calculations.

The gravitic heating hypothesis is distinct and broader. It concerns:

1. **Differential acceleration across density layers** — the liquid outer core, being less viscous than the mantle, responds differently to gravitational perturbations than the surrounding solid material. This differential response generates shear at the core-mantle boundary (CMB).
2. **Gravitomagnetic effects** — under general relativity, the gravitational action of a rotating mass on another rotating mass includes frame-dragging terms (gravitomagnetic effects) that may generate heat in Earth's rotating layers through mechanisms beyond classical tidal deformation.
3. **Variable forcing from planetary configurations** — Jupiter and Saturn perturb Earth's orbital eccentricity on timescales of ~100,000 years. Greater eccentricity means larger variation in the Sun-Earth distance across each orbit, which means larger variation in the gravitational gradient experienced by Earth's interior per orbit.

## 3. Evidence from Solar System Body Research

### 3.1 Io — The Proof of Concept

Jupiter's moon Io is the most volcanically active body in the solar system. Its internal heat source is not radioactive decay — it is gravitational shearing. Io's orbit is kept slightly elliptical by orbital resonances with Europa and Ganymede. As Io moves closer to and farther from Jupiter each orbit, the tidal bulge raised by Jupiter's gravity changes size. The continual flexing of the solid rock generates internal friction and heat, which is released through continuous volcanic activity across the moon's surface.

This is not a theoretical proposition. It is directly observed, thermally measured, and modelled. The mechanism is proven to work at planetary scale.

### 3.2 Europa, Enceladus, and Subsurface Oceans

Europa maintains a liquid subsurface ocean beneath an ice shell despite being far from the Sun. The heat source maintaining this ocean is tidal dissipation from Jupiter's gravity, modulated by orbital resonance with Io and Ganymede. Enceladus (a moon of Saturn) exhibits active geysers driven by tidal heating from Saturn. In both cases, gravitational forcing from an external body maintains liquid water — and habitability — far beyond what solar insolation alone could explain.

### 4.3 The Sun's Barycentric Motion

The Sun does not sit stationary at the centre of the solar system. It orbits the solar system's barycentre — the common centre of mass — in response to the gravitational pull of the planets, primarily Jupiter and Saturn. This motion is measurable and has been tracked. When the outer planets align, their combined gravitational pull shifts the barycentre significantly, and the Sun moves in response.

Earth, orbiting the Sun, therefore experiences a varying gravitational environment as the Sun-barycentre offset changes. The Sun's position relative to the barycentre follows a quasi-periodic pattern with a dominant cycle of approximately 179 years — corresponding to the Jose cycle of Jupiter-Saturn alignment. This means Earth's orbital geometry relative to the true gravitational centre of the solar system is not captured by simple two-body Sun-Earth orbital calculations.

## 4. Evidence from Earth System Research

### 4.1 The 27 TW Thermal Deficit

Published research has proposed that the gravitational action of the Sun on Earth's rotating mass layers — distinct from tidal effects — could contribute approximately 54 TW to Earth's heat budget, which would satisfy the thermal balance and potentially explain the reduced surface heat flow. The effect is predicted to arise from the relativistic (gravitomagnetic) action of the Sun's gravitational field on the rotating masses of Earth's interior layers, which differentially accelerate different density layers, generating heat.

The Moon alone is estimated to generate approximately 1 TW through gravitational action on Earth's interior — ten times greater than conventional solid Earth tidal dissipation. The combined action of Sun and planets on all layers of Earth's interior remains incompletely characterised.

### 4.2 The 6-Year Core-Climate Cycle

A 6-year oscillation has been identified simultaneously in:

- Fluid motions in Earth's outer core
- Earth's internal magnetic field
- Length-of-day variations (Earth's rotation rate)
- Polar motion
- Atmospheric angular momentum
- Global mean sea level rise rate
- Glacier and Greenland ice mass loss rate
- Precipitation patterns
- Land water storage
- Ocean heat content
- Earth's energy imbalance

This 6-year signal propagates from the deepest interior of the planet to the surface climate system. Its period aligns with planetary ephemerides. The physical mechanism coupling core dynamics to surface climate is not fully understood and is not included in any standard climate model.

This is empirical evidence of a direct, active link between Earth's deep interior — where gravitational forcing operates — and the surface climate variables used to assess global warming. It is not a marginal finding; it appears across multiple independent observational systems.

### 4.3 Confirmed Planetary Gravitational Effects on Earth's Orbital History

Geological sediment cores from the time of early dinosaurs (approximately 252–199 million years ago) preserve a record of climate cycles. Analysis reveals a ~1.75 million year cycle in those ancient sediments that corresponds to a 2.4 million year cycle today. The difference is attributed to gravitational interactions between Earth and Mars, which have altered the resonance structure of their mutual orbital perturbations over geological time.

This is direct physical evidence, preserved in rock, that planet-planet gravitational interactions have measurably altered Earth's orbital and climate cycles over time. It is not modelled. No existing calculation precisely reproduces it. The solar system is chaotic over long timescales — Laplace's dream of a single deterministic equation governing all planetary motion was falsified — meaning the orbital environment Earth has experienced over its history cannot be fully reconstructed from current positions and velocities alone.

### 4.4 Core-Mantle Coupling and Earth's Variable Rotation

High-resolution measurements of Earth's rotation rate (length of day) show variations on timescales from sub-annual to decadal that involve coupling between the fluid outer core, the solid mantle, and external forcing including atmospheric winds and ocean currents. The coupling mechanisms — electromagnetic, topographic, gravitational, viscous — are not fully distinguished observationally.

Gravitational coupling between density heterogeneities in the core and the mantle is a known and studied mechanism. Changes in core dynamics on decadal timescales produce measurable gravity anomalies at Earth's surface. The boundary between deep interior dynamics and surface-observable climate effects is not as insulating as standard models assume.

## 5. The Modelling Gap: Why This Forcing Is Missing

### 5.1 It Was Excluded by Assumption, Not by Measurement

The conventional justification for excluding variable gravitational interior forcing from climate models is that tidal dissipation in the solid Earth is approximately 0.1 TW — negligible compared to 173,000 TW of incoming solar radiation. This is technically accurate for the narrow definition of surface tidal dissipation.

However, the relevant question is not whether tidal dissipation is large at the *surface* energy budget level. The relevant question is whether variable gravitational forcing modulates *interior heat output* in ways that affect:

- Mantle convection rates and their effect on continental drift and volcanic outgassing
- Ocean-floor spreading rates and associated submarine volcanism
- Deep ocean CO₂ outgassing from hydrothermal systems
- Core heat flux and its influence on the geodynamo and polar magnetic field geometry

These are not surface energy budget terms. They are slow, deep processes operating on timescales of thousands to millions of years — precisely the timescales on which glacial cycles operate and on which models fail to match the proxy record.

The assumption that these terms are negligible was made for computational tractability in the 1970s and 1980s, when GCMs were first constructed. It has been carried forward by institutional inertia, not by systematic evaluation.

### 5.2 The Holocene Conundrum as the Smoking Gun

If gravitic interior heating varies with orbital eccentricity — which it must, since a more eccentric orbit produces larger variations in Sun-Earth distance and therefore larger variations in the gravitational gradient experienced by Earth's interior — then it would be *highest* during periods of peak eccentricity and *lowest* during circular orbit phases.

The current orbital period is one of unusually low eccentricity. This means Earth is currently experiencing *reduced* gravitational interior heating relative to periods of higher eccentricity during the Pleistocene glacial cycles. Reduced interior heating means reduced mantle heat flux, reduced submarine volcanism, and potentially reduced deep ocean CO₂ outgassing — a background cooling influence operating silently beneath the surface energy budget.

This would provide a physical mechanism for the Holocene cooling trend that the proxy record consistently shows but climate models consistently fail to reproduce. The models exclude the mechanism, so they cannot reproduce the effect.

### 5.3 The 100,000-Year Problem Revisited

The shift from 41,000-year to 100,000-year glacial periodicity approximately one million years ago coincides with no clearly identified change in orbital forcing. However, it does coincide with long-term changes in Earth's average orbital eccentricity driven by the slow evolution of the outer planet configuration over millions of years.

If interior gravitic heating scales with orbital eccentricity, then the shift in mean eccentricity over geological time would change the background level of internal heat output and potentially alter the resonant response of the climate system to orbital forcing — changing which orbital cycle dominates the glacial rhythm. This is a testable hypothesis. It is not currently being tested by mainstream climate science.

## 6. The Epistemic Problem

### 6.1 We Cannot Predict What We Have Not Modelled

The standard conclusion drawn from climate models is that approximately 100% of observed warming since 1850 is attributable to human greenhouse gas emissions, with natural forcings contributing net zero over the same period. This conclusion is derived from models that exclude variable gravitational interior forcing.

If that forcing exists and varies on relevant timescales, the attribution calculation is incomplete. The models would be assigning to greenhouse gases the residual of all unmodelled forcings, not just the forcing due to greenhouse gases.

This is not a claim that human emissions are not warming the planet. The instrumental record since 1850 is precise and unambiguous. The 1.5°C of warming is real. The question is whether the 100% attribution is correct, or whether it is the result of a model that cannot distinguish between CO₂ forcing and unmodelled interior forcing because it was never built to include the latter.

### 6.2 The Mayan Methodological Comparison

The Maya tracked observable astronomical cycles with naked-eye precision and achieved sub-day accuracy on planetary orbital periods extending thousands of years into the future. Their methodology: observe what repeats, record it, extrapolate the pattern. No assumptions about mechanism. No parametrisation of internal processes. Every prediction was falsifiable on the next cycle.

Modern climate science has inverted this methodology. It builds mechanistic models of extraordinary complexity, parametrises processes that are not directly observed (cloud formation, ocean mixing depths, mantle viscosity), and produces outputs that are presented with high confidence despite failing to reproduce the basic direction of temperature change during the Holocene. The mechanisms are specified; the outputs are wrong.

The gravitic heating hypothesis is, in the Mayan spirit, an observation-first proposition: the cycle is not behaving as the models predict; there is a known physical mechanism that operates at scale throughout the solar system; it is not in the models; and the gap between model and observation has the right character to be explained by a forcing that varies with orbital eccentricity on timescales of tens to hundreds of thousands of years.

### 6.3 What We Can and Cannot Honestly Claim

**What is rock solid:**
- The orbital cooling trend (declining boreal summer insolation) is real and has been active for ~7,000 years, documented in 1,310 pollen records
- Earth has an unresolved ~27 TW thermal deficit
- A 6-year cycle connects core dynamics directly to surface climate observables
- Planet-planet gravitational interactions have measurably altered Earth's orbital history (confirmed in geological sediments)
- Tidal heating drives the geological activity of multiple solar system bodies at scales far larger than classical tidal dissipation estimates for Earth
- Climate models produce the wrong sign for Holocene temperature trends compared to proxy data
- The glacial periodicity shift one million years ago has no fully accepted explanation

**What remains unquantified:**
- The magnitude of variable gravitational interior heating on Earth specifically
- Its variation with orbital eccentricity over glacial-interglacial timescales
- The coupling efficiency between interior heat variation and surface climate on relevant timescales
- The proportion of the Holocene cooling trend attributable to this mechanism vs. other unmodelled processes

## 7. Proposed Research Programme

A minimum viable research programme to test this hypothesis would require:

**1. Quantification of variable gravitational forcing on Earth's interior**  
Calculate the differential gravitational acceleration across Earth's density-layered interior as a function of orbital eccentricity, lunar orbital parameters, and outer planet configurations over the past 2 million years. This is a tractable celestial mechanics problem that does not require new physics — only the application of known gravitational theory to Earth's known internal structure.

**2. Correlation with the geological record**  
Compare the calculated forcing history against:
- The proxy temperature record (particularly the Holocene cooling trend)
- The timing of glacial terminations (which remain imperfectly explained by insolation forcing alone)
- The 100,000-year periodicity transition ~1 million years ago
- Periods of enhanced submarine volcanism and deep ocean CO₂ outgassing

**3. Incorporation into GCMs**  
Develop a parametrisation of variable interior heat output as a function of orbital eccentricity and include it in existing Earth System Models. Test whether inclusion of this forcing reduces model-data discrepancy in the Holocene temperature record.

**4. Resolve the thermal deficit**  
Design a dedicated observational programme to characterise the 27 TW thermal deficit — whether it is constant, variable, or correlated with orbital parameters. This would require improved global heat flux measurements, particularly from the ocean floor.

## 8. Summary

The gravitic interior heating hypothesis proposes that the cyclic gravitational interaction between Earth and the Sun, Moon, and outer planets generates variable internal heat through differential forcing across Earth's density-layered interior. This mechanism:

- Is physically real and proven at scale in Io, Europa, and Enceladus
- Has a plausible theoretical basis under both classical and relativistic gravity
- Would produce forcing that varies with orbital eccentricity on precisely the timescales relevant to glacial cycles
- Provides a candidate mechanism for the Holocene cooling trend that models cannot reproduce
- Offers a potential explanation for the 100,000-year periodicity problem
- Is consistent with the observed 27 TW thermal deficit
- Is corroborated by the empirically observed 6-year core-climate coupling signal
- Has never been systematically excluded — it was excluded by assumption during the early development of climate models

The honest scientific position is that a forcing mechanism proven to drive planetary geology throughout the solar system has been assumed to be negligible on Earth without systematic quantitative evaluation, and that this assumption coincides precisely with the timescales and character of the most persistent failures in Earth's climate model record.

The 30-year gap between model outputs and proxy observations is not a calibration problem. It may be a missing physics problem.
