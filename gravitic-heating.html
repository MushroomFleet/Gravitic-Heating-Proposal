Premise

A plumber's problem
on a planetary scale

Consider a simple accounting problem. You are trying to track where the heat in a house is coming from. You measure the boiler output. You calculate losses through the walls. You add everything up — and the numbers don't balance. The house is warmer than your ledger allows. A plumber, faced with this situation, does not conclude that thermodynamics is wrong. A plumber concludes that there is a pipe somewhere that hasn't been found yet.

Earth has a missing pipe.

The planet radiates approximately 47 terawatts of heat from its interior — roughly a thousand times humanity's current electrical generating capacity, flowing continuously upward from the depths of the world. But when scientists tally the known sources of that heat — the slow radioactive decay of uranium, thorium, and potassium buried deep in the mantle — they can account for only about 20 terawatts. The remaining 27 terawatts are real. They are measured. And they have no agreed explanation.

This document explores the hypothesis that the missing heat has been hiding in plain sight — generated by the same mechanism that turns Jupiter's moon Io into the most volcanically active body in the solar system, and that it has been quietly shaping Earth's climate on timescales our models were never built to see.

"We are attributing the present warming primarily to one forcing using models that we know fail to account for all the relevant forces."

The assumption that gravitational interior forcing is negligible was made for computational tractability in the 1970s and 1980s, when general circulation models were first constructed. It has been carried forward by institutional inertia, not by systematic evaluation.

The 0.1 TW tidal dissipation figure applies to surface rock deforming uniformly. The shear at the core-mantle boundary — where a liquid iron layer meets a solid rocky layer under differential gravitational forcing — is a different physical process entirely, and it has not been calculated with comparable rigour.

02 · Earth's Interior

A differentiated body,
layer by layer

Earth is not a solid ball. It is several planets nested inside each other, each with different densities and viscosities — each responding differently to gravitational perturbations from outside.

When the Sun, Moon, or Jupiter pulls gravitationally on Earth, it pulls with a differential force — slightly harder on the near side than the far side. This tidal gradient acts on the whole volume of the planet, but not equally across its layers.

The liquid outer core, being fluid, responds to gravitational perturbation differently from the solid mantle surrounding it. This differential response generates shear at the core-mantle boundary — relative motion between layers of different density and viscosity. Shear generates friction. Friction generates heat.

03 · The Mechanism

How orbital gravity
becomes interior heat

01
Orbital Eccentricity Varies
Jupiter and Saturn perturb Earth's orbit over a ~100,000-year cycle, stretching it from near-circular to more elliptical. A more elliptical orbit means greater variation in Earth's distance from the Sun each year.
02
Tidal Gradient Fluctuates More Severely
Greater orbital eccentricity means larger variation in the gravitational gradient experienced by Earth's interior per orbit. The differential force between Earth's near and far sides to the Sun grows and shrinks more dramatically each year.
03
Differential Acceleration Across Density Layers
Earth's layered interior — with liquid outer core, solid inner core, and viscous mantle — responds differently to the same gravitational perturbation. The liquid core sloshes relative to the surrounding solid rock.
04
Shear at the Core-Mantle Boundary
Relative motion between the liquid core and solid mantle generates shear stress at their boundary. This interface, under enormous pressure at ~2,900 km depth, converts mechanical energy into heat through viscous dissipation.
05
Interior Heat Varies with Orbital Eccentricity
The result: Earth's internal heat output is not constant. It is modulated by orbital eccentricity on precisely the 100,000-year timescale that dominates glacial cycles — a forcing that varies exactly where the climate models fail to match the proxy record.

The mechanism is also expected to operate via gravitomagnetic effects — under general relativity, the gravitational action of a rotating mass on another rotating mass includes frame-dragging terms that may generate additional heat through differential rotation of Earth's interior layers, beyond what classical tidal theory predicts.

A peer-reviewed calculation published in 2013 proposed that this gravitomagnetic action of the Sun on Earth's rotating interior layers alone could contribute approximately 54 terawatts to Earth's heat budget. That figure may or may not be correct — but it was published, and largely set aside without systematic refutation or confirmation.

04 · Proof of Concept

The mechanism is proven
across the solar system

Gravitational interior heating is not theoretical speculation. It is directly observed, thermally measured, and responsible for the geological activity of multiple solar system bodies — at scales far exceeding what classical tidal models predict for Earth.

Io
Jupiter's Moon · Proven Case
The most volcanically active body in the solar system. Its heat source is not radioactive decay — it is gravitational shearing. As Io moves closer and farther from Jupiter each orbit, its tidal bulge grows and shrinks. The continual flexing of solid rock generates friction and heat, released through hundreds of simultaneous volcanoes.
Mechanism: Proven · Thermally Measured
Europa
Jupiter's Moon · Liquid Ocean
Maintains a liquid subsurface ocean beneath an ice shell despite being far from the Sun. The heat source is tidal dissipation from Jupiter's gravity, modulated by orbital resonance. Gravitational forcing maintains liquid water — and habitability — far beyond what solar insolation alone could explain.
Implication: Gravity as primary heat source
Enceladus
Saturn's Moon · Active Geysers
Actively shoots geysers of liquid water hundreds of kilometres into space. The driving force is tidal heating from Saturn. A small icy moon, far from the Sun, sustained as a geologically active ocean world entirely by gravitational energy converted to heat through orbital mechanics.
Implication: Scale far exceeds classical prediction

If orbital gravity turns Io into a volcanic world, keeps Europa's ocean liquid, and drives Enceladus's geysers — all through the same basic mechanism of gravitational differential forcing on a differentiated body — then the question is not whether the mechanism exists on Earth. It must. The question is how large the effect is, and whether it varies on the same timescales as the glacial record.

"A forcing mechanism proven to drive planetary geology throughout the solar system has been assumed negligible on Earth without systematic quantitative evaluation."

05 · The Modelling Gap

Three persistent failures
in 30 years of climate models

Despite decades of refinement, Earth System Models produce systematic errors against the physical record at precisely the timescales where gravitic interior forcing would be expected to operate. These are not calibration errors — they are structural failures indicating missing physics.

The Core Problem · Unresolved since 1990s
The Holocene Temperature Conundrum
Proxy reconstructions from 1,310 pollen records, ocean sediment cores, and lake sediments consistently show the Northern Hemisphere cooling since approximately 7,000 years ago — consistent with declining boreal summer insolation. Climate models simulate the opposite: a long-term warming trend. The two accounts produce opposite signs for temperature change during the same period. This discrepancy has been open and unresolved for over 30 years.
Temporal Shift · ~1 Million Years Ago
The 100,000-Year Problem
For the past 800,000 years, glacial cycles have followed a dominant ~100,000-year periodicity matching Earth's eccentricity cycle. Before approximately 1 million years ago, the dominant periodicity was ~41,000 years, matching obliquity. No identified change in orbital forcing explains this transition. The shift arose from within the Earth system itself — and no existing model satisfactorily explains why it happened.
Measurement Precision · Statistical Validity
The Proxy Uncertainty Floor
The median uncertainty across all Holocene paleoclimate proxy records is ±1.7°C. Individual proxy types reach ±3°C. The entire anthropogenic warming signal attributed to human activity since 1850 is approximately 1.5°C — a value that sits inside the error bar of the measurement tool used to establish its historical context. Peer-reviewed literature has explicitly noted that IPCC statements claiming current temperatures are unprecedented are statistically invalid at this precision.

If gravitic interior heating varies with orbital eccentricity, it would be highest during periods of peak eccentricity and lowest during circular orbit phases. The current orbital period is one of unusually low eccentricity — meaning Earth may be experiencing reduced gravitational interior heating relative to the Pleistocene glacial cycles. Reduced interior heating → reduced mantle heat flux → reduced submarine volcanism → potentially reduced deep ocean CO₂ outgassing: a background cooling influence operating silently beneath the surface energy budget, invisible to models that never included it.

This would provide a physical mechanism for the Holocene cooling trend that the proxy record consistently shows but climate models consistently fail to reproduce. The models exclude the mechanism, so they cannot reproduce the effect.

06 · Empirical Corroboration

The 6-year signal that
no model predicted

In 2023, a series of papers identified a 6-year oscillation appearing simultaneously across a remarkable set of Earth's observable properties — propagating from the deepest interior of the planet to the surface climate system. The period aligns with planetary ephemerides. The physical mechanism is not fully understood, and it is included in no standard climate model.

07 · Epistemic Honesty

What is rock solid —
and what is not

✓   Established Facts
  • The orbital cooling trend (declining boreal summer insolation) has been active for ~7,000 years, documented in 1,310 pollen records
  • Earth has an unresolved ~27 TW thermal deficit — real, published, without accepted explanation
  • A 6-year cycle connects core dynamics directly to surface climate observables across 11 independent datasets
  • Planet-planet gravitational interactions have measurably altered Earth's orbital history, confirmed in geological sediments from 252 million years ago
  • Tidal heating drives the geology of Io, Europa, and Enceladus at scales far larger than classical Earth tidal estimates
  • Climate models produce the wrong sign for Holocene temperature trends vs. proxy data
  • The glacial periodicity shift ~1 million years ago has no fully accepted explanation
~   Unquantified
  • The magnitude of variable gravitational interior heating on Earth specifically
  • The variation of that forcing with orbital eccentricity over glacial-interglacial timescales
  • The coupling efficiency between interior heat variation and surface climate on relevant timescales
  • The proportion of the Holocene cooling trend attributable to this mechanism vs. other unmodelled processes
  • Whether the gravitomagnetic contribution estimate of ~54 TW is correct in order of magnitude
  • Whether the 27 TW deficit is constant, variable, or correlated with orbital parameters

This is not a claim that human emissions are not warming the planet. The instrumental record since 1850 is precise and unambiguous. The ~1.5°C of warming is real. The question is whether 100% attribution to greenhouse gases is correct, or whether it is the result of a model that cannot distinguish between CO₂ forcing and unmodelled interior forcing — because it was never built to include the latter.

08 · What Comes Next

A minimum viable
research programme

Testing this hypothesis does not require new physics. It requires applying known gravitational theory to Earth's known internal structure, over a known orbital history, and comparing the result against a known proxy record. Four tractable steps:

Step 1 · Celestial Mechanics
Quantify Variable Gravitational Forcing
Calculate the differential gravitational acceleration across Earth's density-layered interior as a function of orbital eccentricity, lunar orbital parameters, and outer planet configurations over the past 2 million years. This is tractable without new physics.
Step 2 · Palaeoclimate Correlation
Match Against the Geological Record
Compare the calculated forcing history against the proxy temperature record (particularly the Holocene cooling trend), the timing of glacial terminations, the 100,000-year periodicity transition, and periods of enhanced submarine volcanism.
Step 3 · Climate Modelling
Incorporate into Earth System Models
Develop a parametrisation of variable interior heat output as a function of orbital eccentricity and include it in existing GCMs. Test whether inclusion of this forcing reduces the model-data discrepancy in the Holocene temperature record.
Step 4 · Heat Flux Observation
Resolve the Thermal Deficit
Design an observational programme to characterise the 27 TW deficit — whether it is constant, variable, or correlated with orbital parameters. Requires improved global heat flux measurements, particularly from the ocean floor.
Conclusion

The 30-year gap between model outputs and proxy observations is not a calibration problem. It may be a missing physics problem.


Science advances by finding the things it has left out of the equations. The history of physics is a history of missing terms — each discovered not by doubting what was already known, but by taking seriously the places where what was known failed to match what was measured.


Earth's interior is warm in ways we cannot fully explain. Earth's climate has cooled when our best models say it should have warmed. The core and the climate are oscillating together in ways no model predicted. And across the solar system, on Io and Europa and Enceladus, gravity turns orbital mechanics into fire.


The fire may be here too. We simply haven't looked for it carefully enough.
