### GRAVITIC HEATING

*Grounding Artifact included in repository*

# The Fire Beneath the Equations

*by Isaac Asimov [in the tradition of]*

---

Consider, for a moment, a problem that a plumber would immediately recognise.

You are trying to account for where the heat in a house is coming from. You measure what flows through the boiler. You calculate the losses through the walls. You check the radiators. And when you add everything up, the numbers don't balance — the house is warmer than your accounting allows. A plumber, faced with this situation, does not conclude that the laws of thermodynamics are wrong. A plumber concludes that there is a pipe somewhere that hasn't been found yet.

Earth, it turns out, has a missing pipe.

The planet radiates approximately 47 terawatts of heat from its interior into the oceans and atmosphere. That is a substantial amount of energy — roughly a thousand times the entire current electrical generating capacity of humanity, flowing continuously upward from the depths of the world. But when scientists tally up the known sources of that heat — the slow radioactive decay of uranium, thorium, and potassium buried deep in the mantle and crust — they can account for only about 20 terawatts. The remaining 27 terawatts are real. They are measured. And they have no agreed explanation.

Twenty-seven terawatts of heat, vanishing into the accounting ledger of Earth science, unattributed, for decades.

This is not a minor footnote. It is one of the most consequential missing numbers in our understanding of the planet — and I want to suggest to you that finding where it comes from might require us to look, of all places, at Jupiter.

---

## A Planet That Proves the Point

Before we discuss Earth, let us travel to the most volcanically active world in the solar system. Not Earth, with its impressive but episodic eruptions of Vesuvius and Krakatoa. Not even Io — not yet. Let us start by asking a simple question: what heats a planet's interior?

The standard answer, the one we have carried since the nineteenth century, is two things. First, there is the primordial heat — the residual warmth from when a planet formed, when billions of rocky fragments fell together under gravity and their kinetic energy of motion was converted, by collision, into thermal energy. The planet has been slowly leaking this founding warmth ever since. Second, there is radiogenic heat — the steady ticking of radioactive elements in the rock, converting nuclear binding energy into heat over billions of years.

These two sources suffice, more or less, to explain the geology of the rocky planets. Earth's mantle convects. Volcanoes erupt. The magnetic field churns. The numbers are not perfect, but they are in the right neighbourhood.

Then we looked at Io.

Io is one of the four large moons of Jupiter first seen by Galileo in 1610, those tiny points of light that moved from night to night and convinced him that not everything in the heavens orbited the Earth. The innermost of the Galilean moons, Io is roughly the size of our own Moon. Given its age — the same as the rest of the solar system, approximately 4.5 billion years — it should long since have lost its primordial heat and settled into the quiet, cratered stillness of our own Moon, which stopped being geologically interesting some three billion years ago.

Instead, when the Voyager spacecraft flew past in 1979, scientists found a world in perpetual volcanic eruption. Hundreds of volcanoes, active simultaneously. Vast plains of sulfur and silicate lava. Plumes of material shooting hundreds of kilometres into space. A surface so young, geologically speaking, that impact craters are almost absent — they are buried faster than they form.

Where is this heat coming from?

Not from radioactive decay — there is far too much of it for that. Not from primordial warmth — that ran out long ago. The answer, which took some years to work out fully, is elegant and strange: Io is being squeezed.

Jupiter's enormous gravity pulls at Io, raising a tidal bulge on the side facing the giant planet. If Io orbited in a perfect circle, this bulge would always point at Jupiter and nothing interesting would happen. But Io's orbit is slightly elliptical, kept that way by gravitational resonances with the neighbouring moons Europa and Ganymede. As Io moves closer to Jupiter during one part of its orbit and farther away during another, the tidal bulge grows and shrinks. The solid rock of Io's interior flexes — not much, but steadily, rhythmically, orbit after orbit for billions of years. And that flexing generates friction. That friction generates heat. The heat melts rock. And Io erupts.

This is the mechanism called tidal heating. It is not controversial. It is directly measured and thoroughly understood. It explains not only Io's volcanism but also why Europa, the next moon out, maintains a liquid ocean beneath its icy shell despite receiving only a fraction of the sunlight that bathes Earth — Jupiter's gravitational kneading keeps the interior warm enough for liquid water to persist. Saturn's moon Enceladus, famously, shoots geysers of water into space for the same reason.

Gravity, working on a differentiated body in an elliptical orbit, generates heat. This is demonstrated fact, confirmed by direct observation across multiple bodies in the outer solar system.

Now: why should Earth be exempt?

## The Clockwork We Inherited

To understand why Earth might not be exempt, it helps to understand the true character of the solar system — which is rather more complicated than the elegant orreries of the eighteenth century suggested.

Those beautiful brass mechanical models, with their gears and orbits, embodied a dream: that the solar system is a clockwork, deterministic, its future calculable in principle from its present state. Laplace famously expressed this vision when he wrote that a sufficiently powerful intellect, knowing the position and velocity of every particle in the universe, could calculate the entire future of existence. The solar system, with its planets wheeling in their regular paths, seemed the very image of this dream made real.

The dream was beautiful. It was also wrong.

Over the past century, and with increasing precision over the past few decades, mathematicians and astronomers have established that the solar system is chaotic. Not chaotic in the casual sense of "disordered" — the planets will not fling themselves into random trajectories next Tuesday. But chaotic in the precise mathematical sense: small differences in initial conditions grow exponentially over time, making long-range prediction impossible even in principle. Earth's obliquity — the tilt of its axis — may have varied by tens of degrees over hundreds of millions of years. Mercury's orbit has a small but nonzero probability of becoming unstable within the next five billion years. Geological evidence, preserved in ancient sediments from the age of the first dinosaurs, shows that the resonant interaction between Earth's and Mars's orbits has shifted measurably over a quarter of a billion years.

The solar system is not a clock. It is a jazz ensemble — mostly playing together, but improvising, drifting, responding to each other in ways that compound over time.

Within this ensemble, the dominant voice is Jupiter. A planet more than 300 times the mass of Earth, close enough to exert meaningful gravitational influence on everything inside the asteroid belt. Jupiter perturbs Earth's orbit. It pulls on Earth's eccentricity — the degree to which Earth's orbit departs from a perfect circle — on a timescale of roughly 100,000 years. When the outer planets align favourably, Earth's orbit becomes more elongated. When they align differently, the orbit becomes more circular. This 100,000-year eccentricity cycle is so well established that it is named — it is part of the Milankovitch cycles, the orbital rhythms that have long been known to pace the ice ages.

And here is where the story of Earth's missing heat begins to connect with the story of Io's fire.

If Io generates internal heat because its orbit is elliptical — because the varying distance to Jupiter means varying gravitational gradient, means varying tidal bulge, means friction and warmth — then what happens to Earth when Jupiter pulls its orbit into a more elongated ellipse? Earth, too, experiences greater variation in its distance from the Sun. Earth, too, experiences a gravitational gradient that fluctuates more severely across each orbit. Earth, too, must flex, however slightly.

The conventional answer is: negligibly. The estimate for solid Earth tidal dissipation — the flexing of the rock — is approximately 0.1 terawatts. A rounding error. Not worth modelling.

But this estimate applies to the surface rock. It does not straightforwardly account for what happens deep inside, where Earth is not uniform rock but a differentiated body of astonishing structural complexity.

## The Layers of the World

Earth is not a solid ball. It is, in a sense, several planets nested inside each other, each with different properties, each responding differently to the forces acting on it.

At the centre is the inner core: a solid sphere of iron and nickel approximately the size of the Moon, so compressed by the weight of everything above it that the iron cannot melt even at temperatures exceeding 5,000 degrees Celsius. Surrounding it is the outer core: liquid iron, churning in convective currents that generate Earth's magnetic field, approximately the size of Mars. Above that, the mantle: a rocky layer roughly 2,900 kilometres thick that behaves as a solid on short timescales but as a very viscous fluid over millions of years — think of it as something like warm glass. And at the top, the thin shell of the lithosphere, the crust on which we live.

Now here is the critical point. When the Sun, Moon, or Jupiter pulls gravitationally on Earth, it does not pull uniformly. Gravity falls off with distance. The near side of Earth is slightly closer to the Sun than the far side, and so gravity pulls on it slightly harder. This differential pull — the tidal gradient — acts on the whole volume of the planet.

But it does not act equally on all layers. The liquid outer core, being fluid, responds to gravitational perturbations differently from the solid mantle surrounding it. A differential gravitational acceleration applied to a system where adjacent layers have different densities and different viscosities produces shear — relative motion between layers — at their boundaries. And shear generates heat.

The surface tidal dissipation estimate of 0.1 terawatts is calculated for surface rock deforming uniformly. The shear at the core-mantle boundary, where a liquid iron layer meets a solid rocky layer under differential gravitational forcing, is a different physical process. It has not been calculated with the same thoroughness. It may not be negligible. In fact, a peer-reviewed calculation published in 2013 proposed that the gravitomagnetic action of the Sun on Earth's rotating interior layers alone could contribute approximately 54 terawatts — which would not only explain the 27-terawatt deficit but potentially require revising the internal heat budget upward.

That number may or may not be correct. The point is that it was calculated at all, published in a peer-reviewed journal, and then largely set aside. The field did not rush to refute it or confirm it. It simply passed by, like a remark at a party that no one quite knows how to respond to.

## The Map That Argues With Itself

Meanwhile, something strange has been sitting in the paleoclimate record for thirty years.

The study of ancient climates — paleoclimatology — works by reading the signatures that past temperatures have left in physical materials: the isotopic composition of ice cores drilled from Greenland and Antarctica, the pollen preserved in ancient lake sediments, the chemistry of shells deposited on ocean floors. These proxies are imperfect — each carries uncertainties, biases, calibration challenges — but assembled in their thousands, they tell a consistent story about what Earth's climate has done over the past ten thousand years, the period geologists call the Holocene.

The story the proxies tell is this: Northern Hemisphere temperatures peaked approximately 7,000 years ago, in what is called the Holocene Thermal Maximum, and have been cooling gradually since. This is entirely consistent with orbital mechanics — the current precession cycle means that boreal summer insolation (the intensity of summer sunlight in the Northern Hemisphere, the primary driver of ice age cycles) has been declining for about seven thousand years. The proxy data and the orbital physics agree.

The climate models disagree. When scientists run their best Earth System Models through the Holocene period — the same models used to project future warming — the models produce a long-term *warming* trend over the same period. Not a small discrepancy. Not a matter of magnitude. The models produce the *opposite direction* of temperature change from the one the physical record shows.

This is the Holocene temperature conundrum, and it has been open in the literature since the 1990s. It is not a secret. It is not disputed. It has generated dozens of papers proposing explanations: perhaps the proxies preferentially record summer temperatures and are being misread as annual averages; perhaps seasonal biases in the archive are confounding the signal. These explanations have been tested extensively, and the conundrum remains.

It is, to put it plainly, a model that argues with the world.

Consider what this means for the present situation. The warming observed since 1850 — approximately 1.5 degrees Celsius — is real. The instrumental record is precise and unambiguous. But the context in which that warming is interpreted depends on the models. If the models systematically produce the wrong sign for Holocene temperature trends, then their attribution of present warming — the confident statement that essentially 100% of observed warming since 1850 is due to human greenhouse gas emissions — rests on a foundation that cannot reproduce even the direction of natural climate variation over the preceding ten millennia.

This is not a comfortable observation. It is not an excuse for inaction on emissions. But it is a fact, and it deserves to be stated clearly: we are attributing the present warming primarily to one forcing using models that we know fail to account for all the relevant forces.

## The Signal from the Deep

There is one more piece of evidence that, to my mind, transforms this from an interesting hypothesis into something more urgent.

In 2023, a series of papers identified a 6-year oscillation appearing simultaneously in a remarkable list of Earth's observable properties: fluid motions in the outer core, fluctuations in Earth's magnetic field, variations in the length of a day (Earth's rotation speed, which is measured with extraordinary precision), polar motion, atmospheric angular momentum, global mean sea level rise rate, glacier and Greenland ice mass loss rate, precipitation patterns, ocean heat content, and what researchers call Earth's energy imbalance — the difference between incoming solar radiation and outgoing heat.

All of these variables, from the deepest interior of the planet to the surface weather system, oscillate with the same 6-year period. The period aligns with known patterns in planetary ephemerides — the positions and alignments of the planets.

Think carefully about what this means. The energy imbalance of Earth's surface — the number that climate scientists track as the fingerprint of global warming — is oscillating in synchrony with motions in the liquid iron core, thousands of kilometres below. This is not modelled. This is not predicted by any current climate model. It is simply observed, in the data, waiting for an explanation.

The core and the climate are talking to each other, and nobody knows exactly how.

One possibility — perhaps the most physically natural possibility — is that variable gravitational forcing from the planets modulates core dynamics, which then couple to the overlying mantle and eventually to the surface. The 6-year signal is not the dominant term in the climate record, but it is unambiguously present, and its presence implies a physical pathway between interior dynamics and surface climate that current models simply do not include.

## What the Maya Knew That We Forgot

There is a methodological lesson hiding in all of this that I find worth drawing out.

The ancient Maya were extraordinary astronomers. Without telescopes, without calculus, without any of the theoretical apparatus of modern physics, they tracked the cycles of Venus, Mars, Jupiter, and Saturn with a precision that allowed them to predict planetary positions centuries in advance. Their Dresden Codex contains Venus tables accurate to within a day over five hundred years of observation.

How did they do it? By watching. By recording. By finding the pattern and following it forward. No assumptions about mechanism. No mathematical models of why Venus moved as it did. They did not know — and did not need to know — about gravity or orbital mechanics. They knew the cycle, and the cycle was true.

Modern climate science has inverted this methodology. It builds models of extraordinary mechanistic sophistication — representing cloud droplet formation, ocean mixing layers, ice sheet dynamics, atmospheric chemistry — and uses these models to project future states and infer past ones. The mechanisms are specified in great detail. But when the mechanisms specified are incomplete, the outputs diverge from reality in ways that no amount of tuning can fully correct, because the error is not in the parameters but in the missing equations.

The Holocene conundrum is, in the Mayan sense, a failed prediction. The models say warming. The record says cooling. Seven thousand years of data, preserved in the pollen of 1,310 sites, says the models have missed something.

The question is what. And the answer I have been building toward is this: the models were built in the 1970s and 1980s, when computational resources required drastic simplification, and one of the things that was simplified away — reasonably, given what was known at the time — was the assumption that Earth's interior heat output is constant and the gravitational action of the other planets on Earth's interior is negligible.

The second assumption may be wrong in exactly the way that explains the first failure.

If gravitic interior heating varies with orbital eccentricity — and it must, because greater eccentricity means greater variation in the Sun-Earth distance, means greater variation in the gravitational gradient across Earth's interior, means more shear, more friction, more heat — then we are currently in a period of *reduced* interior heating. Earth's orbit is currently near its most circular in the 100,000-year eccentricity cycle. The interior is being squeezed less than during the periods of high eccentricity that characterised the middle of the last glacial cycle.

Reduced interior heating means reduced mantle heat flux. Reduced mantle heat flux means slower convection, slower seafloor spreading, reduced submarine volcanism, and potentially reduced outgassing of deep ocean CO₂ from hydrothermal systems. These are not surface energy budget terms. They are slow, deep processes, operating on exactly the timescales of glacial cycles — precisely the timescales on which the models fail to match the proxy record.

We may have been looking at the surface of the planet when we should also have been attending to its interior. And the interior, it turns out, is connected to the motions of the planets in ways that are proven throughout the solar system and unquantified on Earth.

## The Missing Pipe

Let me return to where I began: the plumber's problem.

The 27 terawatts of heat flowing from Earth's interior that cannot be explained by radioactive decay is real. It is measured. It has been acknowledged in the literature for years. It is not controversial that it exists — only what it means.

One possibility is that we are underestimating radiogenic heat production. Another is that primordial heat is dissipating more slowly than models suggest. These are the conventional options, and they have been explored.

A third possibility is that some portion of Earth's internal heat is being generated continuously by the gravitational interaction of Earth's layered interior with the varying gravitational fields of the Sun, Moon, and planets — a mechanism proven beyond any doubt in Io, Europa, and Enceladus, present in principle from the same physics applied to Earth, and systematically absent from every climate model currently in use.

The honest scientific position is not that this hypothesis is correct. The honest position is that it has not been properly tested. The gravitational forcing on Earth's differentiated interior has never been calculated with the same rigour applied to the surface tidal effect. The coupling between that forcing and interior heat output has not been parametrised. The variation of that forcing over the 100,000-year eccentricity cycle has not been correlated against the proxy temperature record.

These are tractable calculations. They do not require new physics. They require applying known gravitational theory — the same physics that explains Io — to Earth's known internal structure, over a known orbital history, and comparing the result against a known proxy record.

If the hypothesis fails that test, we will have learned something valuable: that the Holocene conundrum and the thermal deficit must be explained by other means, and we will have narrowed the problem.

If the hypothesis passes, even partially, we will have found the missing pipe. And finding a missing pipe changes everything downstream — including, with appropriate care and epistemic honesty, how we think about the natural variability component of the warming we have observed since 1850.

I want to be clear about what I am and am not suggesting. I am not suggesting that human greenhouse gas emissions are not warming the planet. They are. The physics of the greenhouse effect is as solid as any in atmospheric science, and the measurements since 1850 are unambiguous. What I am suggesting is that a model that cannot reproduce the direction of natural climate variation over ten thousand years may not be correctly partitioning the causes of the variation it observes over 150 years — and that the mechanism most likely responsible for that failure is one that has been assumed negligible without systematic evaluation.

Science advances by finding the things it has left out of the equations. The history of physics is a history of missing terms — the perihelion of Mercury, the expanding universe, the anomalous magnetic moment of the electron — each discovered not by doubting what was already known but by taking seriously the places where what was known failed to match what was measured.

Earth's interior is warm in ways we cannot fully explain. Earth's climate has cooled when our best models say it should have warmed. The core and the climate are oscillating together in ways no model predicted. And across the solar system, on Io and Europa and Enceladus, gravity turns orbital mechanics into fire.

The fire may be here too. We simply haven't looked for it carefully enough.
